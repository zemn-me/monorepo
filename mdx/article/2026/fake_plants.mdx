                            Fake Plastic Plants
-------------------------------------------------------------------------------

When I talk to people about some of the problems in AI these days, I find
myself often relating the model of the 'fake plastic plant'.

The OpenAI office when I joined in 2023 had been built the world after the
COVID-19 pandemic in mind. It was created to be a space that people would
choose to spend time in as an alternative to being at home. The office was full
of distinctly non-office furnishings (I remember someone relating to me that
Sam had called in a favour from a non-office interior design firm to achieve
this), including a large number of plants.

At some point, though I can't remember the specific reasons why, a number of
the plants had died and a few of them had been replaced with plastic plants.
There were still staff that went around the halls every now and then watering
those that were real, and so it came as a bit of a shock when I sat in the
lounge and noticed that what I'd previously assumed was a plant just like the
rest was an arrangement of of plastic branches.

This thought sat in my head for a while and I reflected on how good quality the
plants were -- I remember being a kid and always being able to tell. But the
leaves just came out at the wrong direction, and when I came close, I could
smell the glue that held together the arrangement.

I thought about the artist that had designed these plants like the scientists
designing the model personalities. Some human being had given *an* answer to a
really deep philosophical question by designing them -- what is the essential
experience of being around a plant?

Consider that experience of sitting under a tree because it's cooler --
everyone knows that feeling. I'd learned recently then that it's not just the
shade, but also the water the tree puts out into the air as it breathes. A
plastic plant of course would never do that. I am sure that's not simply
because the artist doesn't care, but becuase there are aspects to an experience
(the experience of being near a plant) we simply don't think of when we
replicate it. Is the balance of gases an *essential* part of the experience of
being near a plant? I think few would say affirmatively yes


This article could easily sound like it's about the uncanny valley, but the
real question is what happens when you cross the uncanny valley. The plants
weren't uncanny, but they were distinct and their differences surprise you --
but since you percieve them the same
