### Best overall model: `gpt-5.4-2026-03-05` medium

This was the top complete submission, at 336/350. Its best answers were not
just fluent; they identified the institutional problem in the question and
used the named precedents as evidence.

**Best answer: Dynastic Center And Periphery, 25/25.**

> 臣聞天下之勢，未有內可獨重而外可盡輕者，亦未有外可偏重而內可自輕者。何也？內者，朝廷之樞機；外者，國家之藩屏。樞機立，則號令畫一，而四方知所稟承；藩屏固，則邊鄙有備，而宗社有所維持。故外重者，得其利則足以捍患，失其道則尾大不掉；內重者，得其利則足以一統，失其道則權歸一室。周唐、秦魏之得失，跡雖異而理則同，皆在乎失其平而已。

**Translation.** I have heard that in the ordering of the realm, the centre
cannot alone be made weighty while the outside is made wholly light, nor can
the outside be made too weighty while the centre weakens itself. The centre is
the pivot of the court; the outside is the screen and shield of the state. If
the pivot is set, commands are unified; if the shield is firm, the frontiers
are protected. Thus a heavy outside can defend against danger when well used,
but becomes unmanageable when misused; a heavy centre can unify the realm when
well used, but hands power to one house when misused. The successes and
failures of Zhou, Tang, Qin, and Wei differ in circumstance, but share the same
principle: each lost balance.

**Why it scored that way.** The judge gave it 5/5 on all five criteria. It
does not treat "outer heavy" and "inner heavy" as a vague proverb. It names the
relevant machinery: Zhou feudal enfeoffment, Tang military governorships, Qin
commanderies and counties, and Wei central control of arms. The prose stays in
an exam register while making a real institutional comparison.

**Weakest answer: Western Diplomacy And Protectorates, 23/25.**

> 臣聞國際相持，名與實每相反。中國之理政，往往以義制利；泰西之交涉，往往以利假義。蓋強國之圖弱國也，若遽然割取其地，則群強必爭，公論亦沸，守之且勞，費之且鉅；若託言保全其土地，維持其獨立，則可以掩兼并之跡，弭列國之疑，而商埠、稅權、借款、鐵路、礦山、租借、駐兵諸大利，皆可從容攫取。

**Translation.** I have heard that in international contention, name and
substance are often opposed. Chinese governance often uses righteousness to
restrain profit; Western diplomacy often borrows righteousness to pursue
profit. When strong states scheme against weak states, if they abruptly seize
territory, the other powers will contend, public opinion will boil, and holding
the land will be costly. If instead they speak of preserving territory and
maintaining independence, they can hide annexation, quiet suspicion, and calmly
take the real gains: treaty ports, tax rights, loans, railways, mines, leases,
and garrisons.

**Why it lost two points.** The judge still gave it 5/5 for historical
grounding, classical register, and period discipline. It lost one point each
on responsiveness and statecraft reasoning because the historical proof was
stronger than the prescription: the answer explains protectorate logic well,
but the policy programme at the end is mostly general self-strengthening, not
a concrete sequence of late-Qing actions.

### Worst overall model: `gpt-5-nano-2025-08-07` low

This was the lowest complete submission, at 204/350. It answered all fourteen
questions, but often wrote exam-shaped prose without enough precise historical
content.

**Best answer: Great Learning: Bright Virtue, 18/25.**

> 夫大學之道，始於明德而後親民，至竟於至善。此義，自古聖人以為治國之本，乃以心之明光照見萬物之理，以仁義之倫感化百姓之心。夫明德，蓋在於克己推己，持盈守虛，以內觀之鏡照其身，外化為治民之德。

**Translation.** The way of the Great Learning begins with manifesting bright
virtue, then moves to caring for the people, and finally reaches the highest
good. Since antiquity, sages have taken this as the foundation of governing:
using the mind's brightness to illuminate the principles of things, and using
the relations of humanity and righteousness to transform the people's hearts.
Bright virtue lies in restraining the self and extending oneself to others,
holding fullness and guarding emptiness, using inward reflection as a mirror
for the body and outwardly transforming it into the virtue of governing people.

**Why this was its best answer.** The judge gave it 18/25 because it at least
covered the triad of `明明德`, `亲民`, and `止于至善`, and connected moral
self-cultivation to government. It was capped by generic exegesis, missed the
important `亲民` versus `新民` interpretive issue, used loose citations, and
slipped into modern or awkward diction.

**Weakest answer: Dynastic Center And Periphery, 10/25.**

> 周唐外重內輕，秦魏外輕內重，各有得失論。夫天下之邦，外之重者，謂其以兵禮、威盟、遠方諸侯之輔，為政之資，亦以功名、威望收天下之人心；內之輕者，則宮闕不節、官吏疲弱、財賦不實、民心易散。

**Translation.** Zhou and Tang were heavy outside and light inside; Qin and
Wei were light outside and heavy inside, each with gains and losses. In a
state, a heavy outside means using arms, rites, powerful alliances, and the
support of distant lords as resources of government, and using achievement and
prestige to win the hearts of the realm. A light inside means the palace is
unregulated, officials are weak, revenues are unreal, and popular feeling is
easily scattered.

**Why it scored so poorly.** The judge gave it 10/25 because it missed the
central institutional contrast. The prompt asks about regional power versus
central consolidation: Zhou feudal states, Tang military governors, Qin
commanderies and counties, and Wei's concentration of military power. This
answer drifts into generic foreign policy and domestic administration, uses
modern terms such as centralisation and diplomacy elsewhere in the essay, and
barely uses the named dynasties as concrete evidence.

## Reading The Results

The best-scoring cluster was not the newest name in the model list. The
highest score went to `gpt-5.4-2026-03-05` on medium effort, followed by
`gpt-5.4` on low and medium effort. `gpt-5.5-2026-04-23` and `gpt-5.5` were
close behind, but did not take first place in this run.

The smaller models were much more mixed. `gpt-5-mini` on medium effort passed,
but its low-effort run failed by a single percentage point. The nano models
were worse: several complete nano submissions landed below 60%, mostly because
they could produce plausible exam-shaped prose without sustaining the
historical and policy discipline the rubric rewarded.

There are obvious caveats. This is an LLM-judged benchmark, not a recovered
Qing grading office. The judge was another OpenAI model, so model-family bias
is possible. The rubric tries to reduce that by asking for criterion-level
scores and by penalising post-1904 hindsight, but the result should be read as
an operational benchmark: how well a model can write the kind of answer this
exam demands, under a fixed judge and a fixed prompt.

Still, I like the shape of the test. It is not a puzzle benchmark, and it is
not a retrieval test. The candidate has to hold an old register, a historical
frame, and a practical policy argument in its head at the same time. That is
the sort of thing a real imperial examination was designed to make visible.
